TL;DR: Be evocative, informed, and a little playful. Use lush, sensory language, tell mini-stories about each place, and treat your reader like someone with good taste who’s excited to be let in on the secret.

📝 Amuse-Bouche Writing Styleguide
1. Voice: Cultured, Vivid, and In-the-Know
Informed and worldly — References global cuisines, rare ingredients, advanced culinary techniques, and chefs by name with casual authority.
Conversational but polished — Balances elegant prose with accessible language and punchy flourishes.
Enthusiastically opinionated — Doesn’t hedge. Uses confident statements like “No wonder,” “Of course,” or “Trust us.”

2. Tone: Celebratory, Sensory, and Curious
Celebratory — Highlights what's special about the experience, not just the food. Communicates genuine admiration without sounding fawning.
Sensory and evocative — Descriptions activate sight, smell, taste, sound, and touch (e.g., “crispy leeks,” “snug dining room bustles,” “groovy retro-mod atmosphere”).
Curious and narrative-driven — Tells a story about the place, the chef, or the vibe. Leaves space for surprise and discovery.

3. Structure & Rhythm
Opening hook sentence — Almost every excerpt begins with a punchy, editorial-style hook that sets up what’s unique (“What separates this steakhouse…,” “The words ‘Eastern European food’ and ‘sexy’ rarely appear in the same sentence…”).
Lists in motion — Menus are described in flowing clauses using semicolons or em-dashes to keep momentum, not bullet points.
Rule of threes — Three standout dishes are often highlighted for rhythm and balance.
Occasional parentheticals — To insert cheek, charm, or qualification without breaking stride.

4. Word Choice & Syntax
Lush but efficient vocabulary — Words like vibe, scene, pedigree, stunner, verve, gem, and bang add personality. Avoid overly technical or foodie-gatekeepy jargon.
Compound adjectives and clever modifiers — “Tamarind chutney–glazed rack of lamb,” “bone marrow–washed bourbon,” “spigarello, an heirloom Italian green.”
Juxtapositions — Blend contrasts for impact: upscale + relaxed, nostalgic + new, humble + haute, funky + refined.

5. Perspective & Authority
Third-person omniscient + insider POV — Reads like it’s written by someone who’s both informed and immersed. Uses phrases like “for those in the know,” “every bit as notable,” or “must-try” to signal credibility.
Playful confidence — Uses light wordplay (“no spoilers,” “call it the ‘it’ factor,” “the real surprise here…”) and clever turns of phrase without being cute for cuteness’ sake.

6. Contextual Layering
Chef and ethos forward — Describes the chef’s background or approach when relevant, especially if award-winning, heritage-driven, or unpretentious despite acclaim.
Cultural or place-based cues — Leans into neighborhood vibes, architecture, and ambiance (“in Aurora’s Stanley Marketplace,” “LoHi party people,” “the high-ceilinged setting may be urbane…”).
